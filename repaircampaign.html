The Repair Campaign is a Caribbean-led advocacy organisation amplifying the global call for reparatory justice — pressing former colonial powers, particularly the United Kingdom, to formally acknowledge their role in the transatlantic trafficking and enslavement of Africans, and to commit to meaningful repair.

When they came to us, they had a mission but no digital home — no place to host their growing community, publish their news coverage, or channel public support into action. We built that from the ground up.

The Challenge

More than a website. A movement infrastructure.

Reparatory justice is a politically charged, historically dense topic. The site needed to do three things simultaneously: inform newcomers who were encountering the movement for the first time, engage existing supporters with fresh content and news, and convert both into active participants — petition signatories, newsletter subscribers, sharers.

The organisation had no in-house technical team. Whatever we built would need to be entirely self-sufficient — the client had to be able to publish a breaking news article, embed a new Instagram post, or update a timeline entry without sending us a message. Any developer dependency would have been a bottleneck on a movement that operates at the speed of news cycles.

There was also the weight of the subject matter. The design couldn't be frivolous or generic. Every decision — typography, spacing, imagery, the tone of CTAs — had to reflect the gravity and legitimacy of the cause.

Key Decisions

The thinking behind the build.

These are the decisions that shaped the project — not just what we chose, but why.

01

WordPress + Elementor over a custom build

This was the foundational decision, and not an obvious one. For a mission-driven organisation with an active news cycle, editorial independence mattered more than technical purity. A custom-coded React or Next.js site would have been faster and more flexible — but it would have made the client dependent on us for every article, every page update, every embed.

WordPress with Elementor gave the Repair Campaign team full control. They can publish breaking news, edit page layouts, add new sections, and update content on their own timeline without a developer in the loop. We built it specifically so they wouldn't need us day-to-day.

The right tool is the one that serves the client's long-term independence, not the one that keeps them coming back to you.

02

A historical timeline at the centre of the experience

The reparations movement is rooted in history. One of the most common barriers to engagement is the feeling that the topic is too complex, too distant, or too contested to navigate. A well-structured timeline — from the transatlantic slave trade through to contemporary CARICOM negotiations — directly addresses that.

We designed the timeline as a visual centrepiece on the homepage, not buried in a sub-page. Two versions were built: a horizontal scrolling layout for desktop that creates a sense of progression through time, and a vertically stacked layout for mobile that respects the reading flow on smaller screens. The goal was to make history feel approachable and navigable — a path through complexity, not a wall of text.

03

Community-first architecture, not broadcast-first

Most advocacy websites default to a broadcast model: they publish information and hope people read it. The Repair Campaign needed something more active. Every page has a conversion point — a newsletter signup, a petition CTA, a "Get Involved" path.

The "Sign in Solidarity" petition was elevated to a primary navigation action, not buried in a page. The newsletter signup appears above the fold and again at the bottom of the homepage. The Instagram feed on the homepage isn't decoration — it shows that this movement is live, active, and growing right now. These are deliberate friction-reduction choices: make it as easy as possible for a visitor to take a step, any step, toward participation.

A signature, a newsletter signup, a share — each is a small commitment that builds toward a larger one. We designed for that ladder, not just for the reader.

04

Separating evergreen content from time-sensitive news

The site serves two very different audiences arriving with different expectations. A first-time visitor needs context: what is reparatory justice, why does it matter, what is the historical basis? A returning community member wants the latest: which governments are moving, what was said at the latest summit, what should they be sharing?

We structured the site accordingly. "Get Informed" and "Learning Hub" house durable, educational content — designed to remain relevant regardless of the news cycle. "In The News," "Dive Deeper," and the newsletter serve the community's need for timely updates. This separation also has an SEO benefit: the evergreen sections build long-term organic visibility around reparations-related search terms, while the news section drives return traffic.

05

Instagram feed integration as a live pulse

The reparations movement is highly active on social platforms — political speeches, breaking news reactions, community updates all flow through Instagram first. Embedding the feed directly on the homepage means the site feels alive even in the gaps between formal blog posts.

It also reduces the content management burden on the team. A single Instagram post automatically surfaces on the website. No duplication of effort, no lag. For an organisation running a lean operation, that kind of workflow efficiency compounds over time.

06

Google Analytics 4 setup and custom dashboarding

Raw analytics data is noise. A dashboard built around the questions an organisation actually asks is signal. We set up GA4 across the site and built custom reporting dashboards that gave the Repair Campaign team immediate visibility into the numbers that mattered to them: traffic sources, most-read content, petition page conversion rates, and — critically — the geographic spread of their audience.

That last point proved strategically valuable. Knowing that significant traffic was arriving from the UK, Jamaica, and Caribbean diaspora communities in North America and Europe helped the team focus their outreach efforts and understand where the movement was resonating. The dashboards were built to be readable by non-technical staff — clear, labelled, and updated automatically.

Analytics only becomes an asset when the people running the organisation can actually read it. We built for the comms team, not for data analysts.

07

SEO structure and article posting strategy

Reparatory justice is an increasingly searched topic — major events like CARICOM negotiations, parliamentary debates, and international advocacy milestones drive significant search spikes. We structured the site to capture that traffic: clean URL slugs, properly configured meta descriptions, schema markup, and content hubs (Learning Hub, Get Informed) deliberately built around long-tail keyword clusters that a first-time searcher would use.

Beyond the technical setup, we established a regular article posting cadence — monthly news roundups (In The News), event-driven articles, and newsletter editions that create a continuous stream of fresh, indexed content. Each new article is an entry point for organic search. Over time this compounded: the site built authority in the reparations topic area, and traffic increased measurably during news cycles as the site ranked for relevant search terms. The SEO work and the community architecture reinforced each other — organic visitors arrived, found value, and converted into subscribers.

08

Duplicate content management

In a content-heavy WordPress site with multiple contributors covering fast-moving news, duplicate content accumulates quickly — similar articles, reposted news items, overlapping topic pages. For most organisations this goes unnoticed. For SEO, it's a quiet tax: Google splits ranking authority across duplicate URLs rather than consolidating it, which means every piece of duplicated content dilutes the equity the site has built.

We conducted an audit of existing content, identified duplicates and near-duplicates, and resolved them through a combination of consolidation (merging overlapping articles), canonical tags (signalling to search engines which version to index), and removal where content was genuinely redundant. We also put in place a lightweight editorial process to prevent recurrence — simple enough that the team could follow it without thinking too hard about it.

09

Email design and campaign management

Email serves a different audience than social media. The newsletter subscriber base for the Repair Campaign skews toward more committed, older supporters — people who opted in explicitly and expect quality content, not just updates. The design and experience of those emails matters.

We designed a branded email template system — header, body, feature blocks, footer — that matched the site's visual identity and held up across email clients (which are notoriously inconsistent with rendering). Beyond the template, we managed the ongoing send cycle: list management, subscriber segmentation, send scheduling, and performance tracking. Consistent, well-designed emails build trust over time in a way that sporadic, generic ones don't. Every send reinforces that this organisation is professional, serious, and worth paying attention to.

10

Custom WordPress plugin: in-admin email scheduling

This was the most technically involved piece of the engagement. The Repair Campaign team found that the workflow friction of switching between WordPress (where they wrote content) and their email platform (where they scheduled sends) was creating delays and errors. Content would be ready but emails would go out late, or drafts would get out of sync between platforms.

We built a custom WordPress plugin that moved the scheduling step directly into the WordPress admin. The workflow became: write the newsletter post in WordPress → set a send date and time in the same editor panel → publish. The plugin handles the rest — queuing the send, pulling the content into the email template, and dispatching at the scheduled time. No platform switching, no copy-pasting, no sync issues.

Building this as a plugin rather than recommending a third-party tool was a deliberate choice. Third-party scheduling tools add another subscription, another login, and another point of failure. A native plugin lives inside the system the team already uses every day and can be maintained and extended as the organisation's needs evolve.

The best tools disappear into the workflow. The team shouldn't have to think about the mechanism — just write, schedule, done.

The Build

What shipped.

The final site comprises a content-rich homepage, dedicated hubs for news (In The News, Newsletter), education (Get Informed, Learning Hub, Dive Deeper), and community action (Get Involved, Sign in Solidarity). The petition integration and newsletter system were wired directly into the site's architecture, not bolted on as afterthoughts.

repaircampaign.org

The site is fully responsive across all breakpoints, with the mobile experience given particular attention given that a significant share of the audience engages via phones — particularly from the Caribbean and UK diaspora communities most active in the movement.

Outcome

Where it landed.

Editorial independence achieved

The team publishes news, schedules emails, and manages all content from a single WordPress admin. Zero ongoing developer dependency.

Measurable traffic growth

The SEO structure and article posting cadence drove compounding organic growth — particularly during news events in the reparations space where the site ranked for relevant search terms.

Analytics-informed decisions

Custom GA4 dashboards gave the team clear visibility into audience geography, content performance, and conversion — turning data into decisions the communications team could actually act on.

Streamlined email workflow

The custom scheduling plugin collapsed a multi-platform workflow into a single step inside WordPress. Newsletters go out on time, on brand, without tool-switching or sync errors.

Clean content architecture

Duplicate content auditing and canonical management protected the site's SEO equity as the content library grew. A sustainable editorial process prevents recurrence.

Active community platform

The petition, newsletter, and Instagram integration run continuously. As the global reparations conversation accelerates, the site is built to scale with it.

The Repair Campaign launched into a moment of growing global attention on reparatory justice, and the site has kept pace — handling increased traffic during news events, growing its subscriber list, and serving as the organisation's authoritative digital home across the UK, Jamaica, and the diaspora.

The cleanest measure of success: since launch, the team has never needed to call us to make a content change.
